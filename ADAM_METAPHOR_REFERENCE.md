**Purpose:** Working reference for ADAM's creative generation, page intelligence, bilateral edge dimensioning, retargeting sequence design, and backfire detection. **Method.** This file was compiled by extracting the full text of the PDF via `pdfplumber` and reading the relevant chapters directly. Lakoff and Johnson established the *conceptual* claim in 1980; Chris's work extends it into the *neural substrate* claim and the *cross-linguistic empirical* claim. The two bodies of work compose.

One metaphor in particular — **PHYSICAL CLOSENESS IS PERSONAL CLOSENESS** (explicit in Ch 15 of MWLB, discussed below) — is directly what Chris's priming experiments have operationalized. The book names it; Chris demonstrated it in the lab.

## 1. The Core Claim (Chapters 1–3)

Lakoff and Johnson argue that metaphor is not a stylistic feature of language. It is the fundamental architecture of thought. Human beings reason about abstract domains (argument, love, time, ideas, morality) by structurally mapping them onto concrete domains (war, journey, money, food, buildings). The metaphor is not a coat of paint on a pre-existing conceptual structure — the metaphor *is* the conceptual structure.

> *"The essence of metaphor is understanding and experiencing one kind of thing in terms of another. It is not that arguments are a subspecies of war. Arguments and wars are different kinds of things—verbal discourse and armed conflict—and the actions performed are different kinds of actions. But ARGUMENT is partially structured, understood, performed, and talked about in terms of WAR."* (Ch 1)

**Three category types** (the book's own organization):

1. **Structural metaphors** (Chapters 1–3, 10) — one concept is metaphorically structured in terms of another (ARGUMENT IS WAR, TIME IS MONEY).
2. **Orientational metaphors** (Chapter 4) — spatial orientations organize whole systems of concepts (HAPPY IS UP, HEALTH IS UP, MORE IS UP).
3. **Ontological metaphors** (Chapter 6) — conceptualizing experiences, events, and abstractions as entities, substances, or containers (THE MIND IS A CONTAINER, INFLATION IS AN ENTITY).

**Personification** (Chapter 7) is a special case of ontological metaphor where the entity is specifically a person. **Metonymy** (Chapter 8) is formally distinct from metaphor — it uses one entity to refer to another related entity rather than structuring one conceptual domain in terms of another — but is treated in the same chapter because the two function similarly in thought.

**Coherence vs. consistency** (Chapter 9). A crucial distinction for ADAM: different metaphors for the same concept (e.g., the several LOVE metaphors, or TIME IS A MOVING OBJECT vs. TIME IS STATIONARY AND WE MOVE THROUGH IT) are not necessarily *consistent* with each other (they don't form a single image), but they can be *coherent* — meaning they share major entailments and "fit together." Lakoff and Johnson argue coherence is more common and more important than consistency.

## 2. Structural Metaphors

Structural metaphors are the highest-leverage category for ADAM because they carry the richest inferential structure. When you invoke a structural metaphor, you import the entire logic of the source domain into the target domain — and that logic is what your creative is actually deploying as a persuasive mechanism.

### 2.1 ARGUMENT IS WAR (Ch 1)

The opening example of the book, given extended treatment. The claim is not that we sometimes talk about arguments using war language but that we literally *think* about arguments in terms of war, and the war structure shapes how we argue.

> *"Your claims are indefensible. He attacked every weak point in my argument. His criticisms were right on target. I demolished his argument. I've never won an argument with him. You disagree? Okay, shoot! If you use that strategy, he'll wipe you out. He shot down all of my arguments."* (Ch 1)

| Linguistic expression | What it imports from WAR |
|---|---|
| "Your claims are indefensible" | arguments have defensive positions |
| "He attacked every weak point" | arguments have vulnerabilities |
| "I demolished his argument" | arguments can be destroyed |
| "He shot down all my arguments" | arguments can be eliminated one by one |
| "I've never won an argument with him" | arguments have winners |

**ADAM relevance** [ADAM analysis]. Every dispute-framing creative (legal, insurance, negotiation, competitive positioning) is operating inside ARGUMENT IS WAR. The metaphor primes adversarial framing, zero-sum thinking, and a need for defensive strength. Effective on prevention-focused users, users high in need for closure, and contexts where trust has already been compromised. **Counter-productive** for cooperative framing, brand-as-partner positioning, and any creative whose target is to reduce conflict or invoke mutual benefit.

### 2.2 TIME IS MONEY, TIME IS A LIMITED RESOURCE, TIME IS A VALUABLE COMMODITY (Ch 2)

Lakoff and Johnson present these as three nested metaphors. TIME IS MONEY is the most specific; TIME IS A LIMITED RESOURCE and TIME IS A VALUABLE COMMODITY are more general categories that TIME IS MONEY specializes.

| Expression | Imported logic |
|---|---|
| "You're wasting my time" | time is a finite quantity |
| "I don't have enough time to spare" | time is budgetable |
| "This gadget will save you hours" | time can be preserved |
| "I've invested a lot of time in her" | time creates expected returns |
| "How do you spend your time these days?" | time is a commodity |
| "That flat tire cost me an hour" | time can be taken by events |

Lakoff and Johnson note that TIME IS MONEY is not universal — it is culturally specific to industrialized societies where labor is paid by the hour, phone bills are metered, and so on. Not every culture conceptualizes time this way.

**ADAM relevance** [ADAM analysis]. Essential for any copy that promises efficiency, speed, productivity, or future-oriented benefit. Primes scarcity, opportunity cost, and the calculating-rational mode of thought. Strongest on achievement-oriented users, promotion-focused regulatory states, and short-temporal-horizon conditions. **Backfires** when the page or user is in a relaxation / escape / leisure mode — invoking time-as-resource when the user has context-switched to time-as-free is a register mismatch that activates the wrong substrate.

### 2.3 TIME IS A MOVING OBJECT; TIME IS STATIONARY AND WE MOVE THROUGH IT (Ch 9)

Lakoff and Johnson use time as their main example of two *different* metaphors for the same concept that are *coherent* with each other without being *consistent*. In one, time is a moving object approaching us from the future; in the other, time is stationary and we move through it toward the future. Both license the front-back orientation of "the future is ahead."

| Metaphor | Expressions |
|---|---|
| **TIME IS A MOVING OBJECT** | "the time will come when...," "the time has long since gone when...," "the time for action has arrived," "time flies," "Christmas is approaching" |
| **TIME IS STATIONARY AND WE MOVE THROUGH IT** | "as we go through the years," "as we go further into the 1980s," "we're approaching the end of the year" |

**ADAM relevance** [ADAM analysis]. Both time metaphors invoke the FRONT = FUTURE orientation, but they license different agency framings. TIME IS A MOVING OBJECT frames the user as a passive recipient of what comes (good for fatalistic, urgency, or inevitability copy). TIME IS STATIONARY AND WE MOVE THROUGH IT frames the user as an active agent traversing time (good for agency, progress, and goal-pursuit copy). A brand can choose which temporal frame to activate by linguistic register alone.

### 2.4 THEORIES (and ARGUMENTS) ARE BUILDINGS (Ch 10)

One of the most extensively documented structural metaphors in the book. An argument or theory has a foundation, is constructed, has supports, can be shored up, can collapse, can be buttressed, has a framework. Lakoff and Johnson note that only the foundation and outer shell of the BUILDING are used in the metaphor — the rooms, staircases, plumbing, and so on are *unused* parts of the metaphor that only appear in creative / poetic extensions.

> *"Is that the foundation for your theory? The theory needs more support. The argument is shaky. We need some more facts or the argument will fall apart. We need to construct a strong argument for that. Here are some more facts to shore up the theory. We need to buttress the theory with solid arguments. The theory will stand on the strength of that argument. The argument collapsed. They exploded his latest theory. We will show that theory to be without foundation. So far we have put together only the framework of the theory."* (Ch 10)

**ADAM relevance** [ADAM analysis]. This is the metaphor field for *any* copy that needs to communicate **solidity, reliability, trustworthiness, and the quality of having been engineered.** It overlaps heavily with the embodied primary metaphor SOLIDITY → TRUST. Strong on users high in uncertainty intolerance, prevention focus, and need for closure. This is the metaphor field that "rock-solid," "foundational," "built to last," "structural integrity," "holds up under scrutiny" all draw from.

**Authority messaging** is often BUILDING-metaphor-implemented. A creative that says "trusted for 20 years" is a weaker version of a creative that says "the bedrock of modern retirement planning" — the first states duration, the second activates the BUILDING substrate that implements the feeling of solidity the user is actually responding to.

### 2.5 The IDEAS cluster (Ch 10)

Lakoff and Johnson give an extended treatment to the many ways IDEAS are conceptualized. They explicitly note that with respect to life and death, IDEAS ARE ORGANISMS, which subdivides into IDEAS ARE PEOPLE and IDEAS ARE PLANTS.

| Metaphor | Example expressions | Entailments |
|---|---|---|
| **IDEAS ARE FOOD** (Ch 2, 10) | "I can't swallow that claim," "let me stew over it," "food for thought," "the meaty part of the paper," "raw facts, half-baked ideas, warmed-over theories," "sink your teeth into," "voracious reader," "devoured the book," "let that idea simmer on the back burner" | ideas are digestible, palatable, nourishing; can be raw, half-baked, fermented, stale |
| **IDEAS ARE PEOPLE** (Ch 10, subcase of IDEAS ARE ORGANISMS) | "the theory of relativity gave birth to many ideas," "cognitive psychology is still in its infancy," "his ideas will live on forever," "that's an idea that ought to be resurrected," "he breathed new life into that idea" | ideas have parentage, life cycles, mortality, resurrection |
| **IDEAS ARE PLANTS** (Ch 10, subcase of IDEAS ARE ORGANISMS) | "his ideas have finally come to fruition," "that idea died on the vine," "a budding theory," "a fertile imagination," "planting a seed of doubt," "barren mind," "come to full flower" | ideas grow organically, require cultivation, can be dormant |
| **IDEAS ARE PRODUCTS** (Ch 10) | "we're churning out new ideas," "he produces ideas at an astounding rate," "needs to be refined," "honed down," "polished" | ideas are manufactured with effort and quality grading |
| **IDEAS ARE COMMODITIES** (Ch 10) | "package your ideas," "he won't buy that," "market for good ideas," "worthless idea," "valuable insight," "wouldn't give a plugged nickel for that idea" | ideas have market value and can be sold |
| **IDEAS ARE RESOURCES** (Ch 10) | "he ran out of ideas," "don't waste your thoughts," "pool our ideas," "used up all our ideas" | ideas are finite and depletable |
| **IDEAS ARE MONEY** (Ch 10) | "let me put in my two cents," "he's rich in ideas," "a treasure trove of ideas," "a wealth of ideas" | ideas can be saved, spent, accumulated |
| **IDEAS ARE CUTTING INSTRUMENTS** (Ch 10) | "an incisive idea," "cuts to the heart of the matter," "a cutting remark," "he's sharp," "a razor wit," "a keen mind," "cut his argument to ribbons" | ideas have sharpness, precision, ability to damage or clarify |
| **IDEAS ARE FASHIONS** (Ch 10) | "that idea went out of style," "in these days," "currently fashionable in Western Europe," "old hat," "outdated," "avant-garde," "in vogue" | ideas have cyclical social currency |

Additionally:

- **UNDERSTANDING IS SEEING; IDEAS ARE LIGHT-SOURCES; DISCOURSE IS A LIGHT-MEDIUM** (Ch 10). A linked triple. *"I see what you're saying. It looks different from my point of view. What is your outlook? I view it differently. Now I've got the whole picture. Let me point something out. That's an insightful idea. That was a brilliant remark. The argument is clear. It was a murky discussion. A transparent argument. The discussion was opaque."*

- **IDEAS ARE OBJECTS** and **MEANINGS ARE OBJECTS** (Ch 2, 10) — the foundation for the CONDUIT metaphor below.

**ADAM relevance** [ADAM analysis]. Copy that describes your brand's *ideas*, *approach*, *philosophy*, or *methodology* is implicitly choosing one of these mappings, and the choice determines which social-emotional substrate gets activated.

- IDEAS ARE PLANTS → cultivation, patience, organic growth — wellness, sustainability, education, craft brands
- IDEAS ARE PRODUCTS → precision, engineering, quality control — technical, B2B, premium positioning
- IDEAS ARE PEOPLE → empathy, care, relationship — services with ongoing client relationships
- IDEAS ARE FOOD → approachability, appetite, bodily satisfaction — content platforms, media, lifestyle
- IDEAS ARE MONEY → value, calculation, ROI — finance, investment, efficiency pitches
- IDEAS ARE CUTTING INSTRUMENTS → sharpness, precision, analytical power — analytics, consulting, decision-support tools

A brand using IDEAS ARE FOOD language for an audience primed by the page into IDEAS ARE PRODUCTS mode is committing a substrate mismatch. The words will land as tonally off without the reader being able to articulate why.

### 2.6 The CONDUIT metaphor for communication (Ch 2, 10, 27)

Reddy's conduit metaphor, given extended treatment. It has three composed parts:

1. **IDEAS (OR MEANINGS) ARE OBJECTS**
2. **LINGUISTIC EXPRESSIONS ARE CONTAINERS (FOR MEANINGS)**
3. **COMMUNICATION IS SENDING**

The composite: ideas are objects, which the speaker packages into containers (words/sentences) and sends through a conduit to the listener, who unpacks them.

| Expression | What it imports |
|---|---|
| "It's hard to get that idea across to him" | ideas travel through space |
| "Your reasons came through to us" | ideas move from speaker to listener |
| "Try to pack more thought into fewer words" | words are containers with capacity |
| "I can't put that idea into words" | ideas exist independent of words, get packed into them |
| "That sentence carries little meaning" | words bear loads of meaning |
| "The meaning is right there in the words" | meaning is locatable inside the container |

**ADAM relevance** [ADAM analysis]. This is the metaphor most advertising copywriters are unconsciously operating inside. It presumes the job of creative is to package an idea and ship it to the user. **The Bargh / embodied frame says this is wrong** — advertising does not transmit ideas, it *activates substrates*. But the conduit metaphor is so entrenched in English that the wrong frame feels natural. Worth being aware of explicitly so we can catch ourselves using it, especially when talking about "messaging strategy" or "getting the value proposition across." Every time someone on the team says those words, they are importing the conduit metaphor's assumptions, and those assumptions are not the assumptions ADAM is built on.

### 2.7 The LOVE cluster (Ch 10, 18)

Lakoff and Johnson use LOVE as their main demonstration that the same target domain can be conceptualized by multiple incompatible but coherent structural metaphors, each licensing different entailments and primed by different contexts.

| Metaphor | Example expressions | What it licenses |
|---|---|---|
| **LOVE IS A JOURNEY** (Ch 9, 10) | "look how far we've come," "we're at a crossroads," "go our separate ways," "we can't turn back now," "this relationship isn't going anywhere," "where are we?," "we're stuck," "a long, bumpy road," "dead-end street," "spinning our wheels," "the marriage is on the rocks," "we've gotten off the track" | progression, shared destination, milestones, obstacles, drift |
| **LOVE IS A PHYSICAL FORCE (ELECTROMAGNETIC, GRAVITATIONAL, etc.)** (Ch 10) | "I could feel the electricity between us," "there were sparks," "magnetically drawn to her," "uncontrollably attracted to each other," "gravitated to each other immediately," "revolves around her," "charged atmosphere," "incredible energy," "lost their momentum" | involuntariness, natural law, physics-level compulsion |
| **LOVE IS A PATIENT** (Ch 10) | "this is a sick relationship," "strong, healthy marriage," "the marriage is dead—it can't be revived," "their marriage is on the mend," "getting back on our feet," "in really good shape," "listless marriage," "tired affair," "on its last legs" | care, diagnosis, recovery, mortality |
| **LOVE IS MADNESS** (Ch 10) | "I'm crazy about her," "she drives me out of my mind," "he constantly raves about her," "gone mad over her," "wild about Harry," "insane about her" | loss of rational control, obsession |
| **LOVE IS MAGIC** (Ch 10) | "she cast her spell over me," "the magic is gone," "spellbound," "had me hypnotized," "in a trance," "entranced," "charmed by her," "bewitching" | supernatural explanation, inexplicability, fragility |
| **LOVE IS WAR** (Ch 10) | "his many rapid conquests," "she fought for him, but his mistress won out," "he fled from her advances," "she pursued him relentlessly," "gaining ground," "won her hand in marriage," "overpowered her," "besieged by suitors," "enlisted the aid of her friends," "made an ally of her mother," "misalliance" | adversarial, strategic, conquest-framed |

Lakoff and Johnson also note that all of these are subcategorizations of the more general **LOVE IS AN EMOTION** and that the various love metaphors are not *consistent* (they do not form one image) but are *coherent* (they share major entailments).

**ADAM relevance** [ADAM analysis]. Relationship-framing creatives — dating, luxury, loyalty programs, long-term service brands — are choosing a LOVE metaphor whether they realize it or not. The choice determines the substrate activated.

- LOVE IS A JOURNEY → long-term loyalty brands, travel, lifestyle transitions, luxury positioning (**LUXY Ride is probably mostly in this metaphor** — it is literally a journey service, so the metaphorical and literal frames align, which is a rare alignment worth exploiting)
- LOVE IS A PHYSICAL FORCE → impulse purchase, immediate chemistry, attraction-based framing
- LOVE IS A PATIENT → wellness brands, relationship counseling, health-adjacent services
- LOVE IS MAGIC → luxury, experience marketing, ineffable-quality positioning
- LOVE IS MADNESS → lifestyle extremism, aspirational intensity, fandom cultivation

A luxury brand running a LOVE IS A JOURNEY campaign on a page priming LOVE IS MAGIC mindset is underperforming the substrate alignment it could have achieved. Both metaphors feel like love talk — one is building a partner, the other is invoking enchantment — and which substrate is active on the page determines which creative lands.

### 2.8 The ARGUMENT cluster continued (Ch 17)

Lakoff and Johnson note in Chapter 17 that ARGUMENT is not only structured by ARGUMENT IS WAR but also by AN ARGUMENT IS A JOURNEY and AN ARGUMENT IS A CONTAINER:

- **AN ARGUMENT IS A JOURNEY** — "we have set out to prove...," "when we get to the next point...," "so far we've seen that...," "we will proceed in a step-by-step fashion," "I got lost in the argument"
- **AN ARGUMENT IS A CONTAINER** — "that argument has holes in it," "your point is well-contained," "the content of your argument"

These compose with ARGUMENT IS WAR without being inconsistent — they share the major entailment that arguments have *structure* that can be inspected.

### 2.9 LIFE metaphors (Ch 10)

- **LIFE IS A CONTAINER** — "I've had a full life," "life is empty for him," "there's not much left for him in life," "her life is crammed with activities," "get the most out of life," "his life contained a great deal of sorrow," "live your life to the fullest"
- **LIFE IS A GAMBLING GAME** — "I'll take my chances," "the odds are against me," "an ace up my sleeve," "he's holding all the aces," "it's a toss-up," "if you play your cards right," "he won big," "he's a real loser," "the chips are down," "ace in the hole," "he's bluffing," "let's up the ante," "sweeten the pot," "stand pat," "the luck of the draw," "high stakes"

Lakoff and Johnson note that LIFE IS A GAMBLING GAME is an example of a metaphor composed almost entirely of "phrasal lexical items" — fixed expressions that function like single words. *"If you say 'The odds are against us' or 'We'll have to take our chances,' you would not be viewed as speaking metaphorically but as using the normal everyday language appropriate to the situation. Nevertheless, your way of talking about, conceiving, and even experiencing your situation would be metaphorically structured."*

**ADAM relevance** [ADAM analysis]. LIFE IS A GAMBLING GAME is powerful for any copy that needs to invoke risk, uncertainty, stakes, or big-bet framing. Strong on exploration-oriented users, high-information-value contexts, and promotion-focused users. It is also one of the most unconscious metaphors in the book — users will not realize they are being primed into a gambling frame, which makes it high-leverage and also carries ethical weight, because it overlaps with gambling-adjacent psychological vulnerabilities.

### 2.10 Other notable structural metaphors (Ch 10)

- **WEALTH IS A HIDDEN OBJECT** — "seeking his fortune," "flaunting his newfound wealth," "fortune-hunter," "gold-digger," "lost his fortune," "searching for wealth." Licenses discovery and exploration framing for investment, opportunity, and "find what you've been missing" creatives.

- **SIGNIFICANT IS BIG** — "he's a big man in the garment industry," "a giant among writers," "the biggest idea to hit advertising in years," "head and shoulders above everyone," "only a small crime," "only a little white lie," "the enormity of the crime," "one of the greatest moments," "towers over those of lesser men." Licenses scale claims in copy; related to MORE IS UP (orientational).

- **SEEING IS TOUCHING; EYES ARE LIMBS** (Ch 10) — "I can't take my eyes off her," "sits with his eyes glued to the TV," "her eyes picked out every detail," "their eyes met," "she ran her eyes over everything in the room," "he wants everything within reach of his eyes." Licenses sensory, tactile language for perception-heavy products (fashion, design, art, food, experience marketing).

- **THE EYES ARE CONTAINERS FOR THE EMOTIONS** (Ch 10) — "I could see the fear in his eyes," "his eyes were filled with anger," "there was passion in her eyes," "his eyes displayed his compassion," "love showed in his eyes." Licenses emotional-intensity visual framing; pairs well with photography-led creative that foregrounds faces and gaze.

- **EMOTIONAL EFFECT IS PHYSICAL CONTACT** (Ch 10) — *"his mother's death hit him hard. That idea bowled me over. She's a knockout. I was struck by his sincerity. That really made an impression on me. He made his mark on the world. I was touched by his remark. That blew me away."* **This is one of the most directly relevant metaphors for Chris's research on physical-to-social substrate recycling.** The metaphor licenses language where emotional impact is literally physical contact — touch, strike, hit, push, bowl over. Copy that invokes tactile verbs for emotional outcomes is priming the embodied substrate directly.

- **LABOR IS A RESOURCE** (Ch 25) — "labor costs," "human resources," "labor supply," "productive labor." Lakoff and Johnson's extended treatment of this metaphor in their chapter on truth notes that LABOR IS A RESOURCE *hides* the distinction between meaningful and dehumanizing labor, because resources are fungible and undifferentiated. *"For all of the labor statistics, there is none on meaningful labor."* A rare case where the book explicitly discusses how a metaphor's *hiding* effect has political and moral consequences.

## 3. Orientational Metaphors (Chapter 4)

Orientational metaphors are the ones most directly rooted in bodily experience. They arise because we have bodies with fronts, backs, tops, bottoms; stand upright; move forward; lie down when sick; pile more substance higher. Lakoff and Johnson argue these are not arbitrary — they have physical and cultural bases that are near-universal, though *which* specific orientations get activated and with what priority varies somewhat across cultures.

For ADAM, orientational metaphors are the most directly **extractable from RSS-level page text** because they manifest as specific directional vocabulary that is cheap to detect. They are also the easiest to extract from user review language.

### 3.1 The UP/DOWN cluster — the ten major metaphors (Ch 4)

Lakoff and Johnson enumerate ten major UP/DOWN orientational metaphors, each with a specific physical or cultural basis. I am reproducing them in the order and phrasing of Chapter 4.

| Metaphor | Expressions | Physical basis (book's own) |
|---|---|---|
| **HAPPY IS UP; SAD IS DOWN** | "I'm feeling up," "that boosted my spirits," "my spirits rose," "in high spirits," "gives me a lift" / "feeling down," "depressed," "really low," "fell into a depression," "my spirits sank" | Drooping posture typically goes along with sadness and depression, erect posture with a positive emotional state |
| **CONSCIOUS IS UP; UNCONSCIOUS IS DOWN** | "get up," "wake up," "he rises early" / "fell asleep," "dropped off to sleep," "under hypnosis," "sank into a coma" | Humans and most other mammals sleep lying down and stand up when they awaken |
| **HEALTH AND LIFE ARE UP; SICKNESS AND DEATH ARE DOWN** | "at the peak of health," "Lazarus rose from the dead," "in top shape," "way up there" / "fell ill," "sinking fast," "came down with the flu," "his health is declining," "dropped dead" | Serious illness forces us to lie down physically; when you're dead, you are physically down |
| **HAVING CONTROL or FORCE IS UP; BEING SUBJECT TO CONTROL or FORCE IS DOWN** | "I have control over her," "on top of the situation," "superior position," "at the height of his power," "high command," "upper echelon," "ranks above me" / "under my control," "fell from power," "on the decline," "social inferior," "low man on the totem pole" | Physical size correlates with physical strength, and the victor in a fight is typically on top |
| **MORE IS UP; LESS IS DOWN** | "the number keeps going up," "his draft number is high," "my income rose," "gone down in the past year" / "incredibly low," "his income fell," "he is underage," "turn the heat down" | If you add more of a substance to a container or pile, the level goes up |
| **FORESEEABLE FUTURE EVENTS ARE UP (and AHEAD)** | "all upcoming events are listed in the paper," "what's coming up this week?," "I'm afraid of what's up ahead," "what's up?" | Our eyes look in the direction in which we typically move; an approaching object appears larger and its top appears to move upward in our field of vision |
| **HIGH STATUS IS UP; LOW STATUS IS DOWN** | "a lofty position," "she'll rise to the top," "at the peak of his career," "climbing the ladder" / "little upward mobility," "at the bottom of the social hierarchy," "fell in status" | Status correlates with social power, and social power is physical power-up |
| **GOOD IS UP; BAD IS DOWN** | "things are looking up," "we hit a peak last year," "high-quality work" / "downhill ever since," "at an all-time low" | Happiness, health, life, and control — all things that characterize what is good for a person — are all UP |
| **VIRTUE IS UP; DEPRAVITY IS DOWN** | "high-minded," "high standards," "upright," "upstanding citizen" / "a low trick," "don't be underhanded," "wouldn't stoop to that," "beneath me," "fell into the abyss of depravity," "low-down thing to do" | GOOD IS UP for a person, combined with SOCIETY IS A PERSON — virtuous actions correlate with social well-being |
| **RATIONAL IS UP; EMOTIONAL IS DOWN** | "the discussion fell to the emotional level, but I raised it back up to the rational plane," "we put our feelings aside and had a high-level intellectual discussion," "he couldn't rise above his emotions" | In our culture people view themselves as in control over animals, plants, and their physical environment, and it is their unique ability to reason that places them above; CONTROL IS UP thus provides MAN IS UP and therefore RATIONAL IS UP |

**The book also notes:**

- **HAPPY IS WIDE; SAD IS NARROW** is a minor alternative to HAPPY IS UP — "I'm feeling expansive" picks out a different aspect of happiness than "I'm feeling up." HAPPY IS UP is the dominant version in English because it is maximally coherent with GOOD IS UP, HEALTHY IS UP, etc.

- **ACTIVE IS UP; PASSIVE IS DOWN** is mentioned at the end of Chapter 5 as a nonspatial orientation that is treated spatially in English but not in all cultures.

- **UNKNOWN IS UP; KNOWN IS DOWN** (Ch 4) is an apparent exception that Lakoff and Johnson specifically flag. Examples: "that's up in the air," "the matter is settled." They argue this does not contradict GOOD IS UP because it has a *different experiential basis* — it is coherent with UNDERSTANDING IS GRASPING ("it's easier to understand something if you can hold it in your hand on the ground than if it's floating through the air"). This is an important point for ADAM: metaphors that appear to contradict each other may be coherent by virtue of having different experiential bases, not because they form one consistent image.

- **FINISHED IS UP** ("I'm finishing up," "wrap it up") is also mentioned, with the same caveat — it does not conflict with UNKNOWN IS UP despite seeming like it should, because they rest on different grounds.

- **MUNDANE REALITY IS DOWN** ("down to earth," "low-level phonology") — the reason why "low-level" in a technical context means "closer to the physical substrate."

- **THE FUTURE IS UP** is noted specifically in Ch 5 as being coherent with "the future will be better" (which is itself coherent with GOOD IS UP).

- **NEAREST IS FIRST** — an orientational metaphor governing linear ordering.

**ADAM relevance** [ADAM analysis]. Nearly all self-improvement, aspiration, achievement, and status-signaling creatives are implicitly working in UP/DOWN space. Any copy using *rise, climb, ascend, reach, peak, top, summit, elevate, lift, raise, higher, above, beyond* is invoking the UP cluster. Any copy using *avoid the low, don't fall, pick yourself up, never down* is invoking the DOWN avoidance. Testable extraction: count up/down vocabulary density in pages and user reviews as a proxy for activated orientation.

**Cultural values anchored in UP/DOWN** (Ch 5):

- *"More is better"* — coherent with MORE IS UP + GOOD IS UP
- *"Bigger is better"* — coherent with MORE IS UP + GOOD IS UP
- *"The future will be better"* — coherent with THE FUTURE IS UP + GOOD IS UP
- *"There will be more in the future"* — coherent with MORE IS UP + THE FUTURE IS UP
- *"Your status should be higher in the future"* — coherent with HIGH STATUS IS UP + THE FUTURE IS UP

These are **the values deeply embedded in mainstream Western culture, all of which are coherent with the UP/DOWN orientational system.** Subcultures (the book cites Trappists as an example) can override priorities — LESS IS BETTER can hold for material possessions in a monastic order — but the UP orientation is preserved at a different level (virtue, salvation) rather than discarded. This is exactly what primary metaphor theory predicts: the substrate is constant, only the targets change.

### 3.2 Container / IN-OUT orientation (Ch 4, 6)

Lakoff and Johnson note IN/OUT as one of the major orientational axes, but most of their treatment of container metaphors appears in the ontological chapter (Ch 6). See Section 4.3 below for the full treatment.

### 3.3 FRONT/BACK (Ch 4, 9)

Treated extensively in Chapter 9 via the TIME examples. Key points:

- Future is typically in front and past is behind in English (**FUTURE IS AHEAD**)
- This is not universal — the book notes cultures where the orientation is reversed (Hausa is given as an example elsewhere)
- Moving objects have fronts in the direction of motion, which is why time can be said to have a face ("I can't face the future")

### 3.4 Non-UP/DOWN orientations noted in the book

- **DEEP/SHALLOW** — "a deep thought," "shallow argument," "deeply moved"
- **CENTRAL/PERIPHERAL** — "the heart of the matter," "central to our mission," "peripheral concerns"

These receive less extended treatment than UP/DOWN but are given as examples of orientational axes.

## 4. Ontological Metaphors (Chapter 6)

Ontological metaphors turn experiences, events, activities, emotions, and ideas into *things* — entities, substances, containers — so that we can refer to them, quantify them, reason about them, and identify their parts. Lakoff and Johnson argue this is the most basic metaphorical move, because without it we cannot even think about abstractions as discrete objects of thought.

> *"Understanding our experiences in terms of objects and substances allows us to pick out parts of our experience and treat them as discrete entities or substances of a uniform kind. Once we can identify our experiences as entities or substances, we can refer to them, categorize them, group them, and quantify them — and, by this means, reason about them."* (Ch 6)

### 4.1 Entity and substance metaphors (Ch 6)

The most basic ontological move: turning an abstraction into a thing.

- **INFLATION IS AN ENTITY** — "inflation is lowering our standard of living," "if there's much more inflation, we'll never survive," "we need to combat inflation," "inflation is backing us into a corner," "inflation is taking its toll at the checkout counter," "buying land is the best way of dealing with inflation," "inflation makes me sick." This is the book's primary example of how an entity metaphor enables referring, quantifying, identifying causes, setting goals, and acting with respect to an abstraction.

- **INFLATION IS AN ADVERSARY** (Ch 7, personification subcase) — a more specific elaboration: "inflation has attacked the foundation of our economy," "inflation has pinned us to the wall," "our biggest enemy right now is inflation," "the dollar has been destroyed by inflation," "inflation has robbed me," "inflation has outwitted the best economic minds." Lakoff and Johnson note that this specific personification *justifies political actions* — declaring war on inflation, setting targets, calling for sacrifices. **This is a direct demonstration of how a metaphor's choice has real-world policy consequences.**

Lakoff and Johnson's list of purposes that ontological metaphors serve (Ch 6):

1. **Referring** — "my fear of insects is driving my wife crazy"
2. **Quantifying** — "it will take a lot of patience to finish this book"
3. **Identifying aspects** — "the ugly side of his personality comes out under pressure"
4. **Identifying causes** — "the pressure of his responsibilities caused his breakdown"
5. **Setting goals and motivating actions** — "he went to New York to seek fame and fortune"

### 4.2 THE MIND IS AN ENTITY and its elaborations (Ch 6)

THE MIND IS AN ENTITY is elaborated in two specific and incompatible ways in English:

| Metaphor | Expressions | Entailment |
|---|---|---|
| **THE MIND IS A MACHINE** | "we're still trying to grind out the solution," "my mind just isn't operating today," "boy, the wheels are turning now!," "I'm a little rusty today," "we've been working on this problem all day and now we're running out of steam" | Mind has on-off state, level of efficiency, productive capacity, internal mechanism, source of energy, operating condition |
| **THE MIND IS A BRITTLE OBJECT** | "her ego is very fragile," "you have to handle him with care since his wife's death," "he broke under cross-examination," "she is easily crushed," "the experience shattered him," "I'm going to pieces," "his mind snapped" | Mind can be damaged by impact, needs protection, is delicate |

Lakoff and Johnson note that these two metaphors are not exactly equivalent: "He broke down" (MACHINE) and "He cracked up" (BRITTLE OBJECT) describe different kinds of failure modes. The MACHINE metaphor describes something that simply ceases to function; the BRITTLE OBJECT metaphor describes something that shatters with dangerous consequences. *"When someone goes crazy and becomes wild or violent, it would be appropriate to say 'He cracked up.' On the other hand, if someone becomes lethargic and unable to function for psychological reasons, we would be more likely to say 'He broke down.'"*

- **THE MIND IS A CONTAINER** (Ch 6, 27) — added explicitly later in the book. "Full of ideas," "empty-headed," "get that out of your head." This is the third major MIND metaphor, combining with IDEAS ARE OBJECTS and the CONDUIT metaphor to create the standard Western conception of communication.

**ADAM relevance** [ADAM analysis]. Copy that treats the mind as a MACHINE licenses performance, optimization, productivity, and efficiency framing — biohacking, nootropics, productivity tools, workflow software. Copy that treats the mind as a BRITTLE OBJECT licenses protection, care, fragility framing — therapy, meditation apps, wellness, anti-burnout messaging. Mismatching these is a substrate error: a brain-training app that invokes brittleness is priming the wrong frame and will underperform a version that invokes the machine frame. A therapy app that invokes the machine frame is cold and inhuman where it needs to be caring. The MIND IS A CONTAINER frame is the default that most ad copy unconsciously invokes — think of any "fill your mind with" or "clear your mind of" line.

### 4.3 Container metaphors (Ch 6)

Lakoff and Johnson devote a major section of Chapter 6 to container metaphors because they argue humans project container-hood onto almost everything.

> *"We are physical beings, bounded and set off from the rest of the world by the surface of our skins, and we experience the rest of the world as outside us. Each of us is a container, with a bounding surface and an in-out orientation. We project our own in-out orientation onto other physical objects that are bounded by surfaces."* (Ch 6)

**Specific container metaphors enumerated in Chapter 6:**

- **LAND AREAS ARE CONTAINERS** — "there's a lot of land in Kansas," "moving out of one room and into another," "a clearing in the woods"
- **VISUAL FIELDS ARE CONTAINERS** — "the ship is coming into view," "I have him in sight," "the tree is in the way," "he's out of sight now," "nothing in sight"
- **EVENTS ARE CONTAINER OBJECTS** — "are you in the race on Sunday?," "halfway into the race, I ran out of energy," "he's out of the race now"
- **ACTIONS / ACTIVITIES ARE SUBSTANCES IN CONTAINERS** — "in washing the window, I splashed water all over the floor," "how did Jerry get out of washing the windows?," "outside of washing the windows, what else did you do?," "I put a lot of energy into washing the windows," "I get a lot of satisfaction out of washing windows"
- **STATES ARE CONTAINERS** — "he's in love," "we're out of trouble now," "he's coming out of the coma," "I'm slowly getting into shape," "he entered a state of euphoria," "he fell into a depression"
- **LIFE IS A CONTAINER** (from Ch 10 further examples) — "I've had a full life," "life is empty for him," "get the most out of life"
- **SOCIAL GROUPS ARE CONTAINERS** — implied throughout, and directly relevant to any creative invoking belonging or membership

**ADAM relevance** [ADAM analysis]. Container-activation is one of the easiest and most universal primes, and extracting container-vocabulary density from pages is a high-signal low-cost NLP task. Container-based creatives are high-leverage for subscription services, loyalty programs, community platforms, insurance, "inside access" framing, membership programs, and luxury "members only" positioning. Any copy using *inside, within, enclosed, join, enter, member, belonging, surrounded, wrapped, community* is container-priming.

### 4.4 Other ontological metaphors

- **PHYSICAL AND EMOTIONAL STATES ARE ENTITIES WITHIN A PERSON** (Ch 10) — "he has a pain in his shoulder," "my cold has gone from my head to my chest," "his pains went away," "his depression returned," "hot tea and honey will get rid of your cough," "he could barely contain his joy," "wipe that sneer off your face, private!," "his fears keep coming back," "I've got to shake off this depression—it keeps hanging on," "if you've got a cold, drinking lots of tea will flush it out of your system," "there isn't a trace of cowardice in him," "he hasn't got an honest bone in his body." This is a big one: **internal states (both physical and emotional) are treated as entities that can be located, moved, extracted, expelled, hidden, or come and go.** It is the substrate for "flush out the toxins," "release your anger," "hold on to happiness," "let go of fear."

- **EVENTS ARE OBJECTS** — implicit in much of the book; made explicit in the grep. "Catch the event," "the event passed," "a beautiful catch"

## 5. Personification (Chapter 7)

Personification is a special case of ontological metaphor where the non-human entity is specifically conceptualized as a person. Lakoff and Johnson stress that personification is *not a single unified process* — each personification picks out different aspects of what a person is and licenses different inferences.

Example list from Chapter 7:

- "His theory explained to me the behavior of chickens raised in factories"
- "This fact argues against the standard theories"
- "Life has cheated me"
- "Inflation is eating up our profits"
- "His religion tells him that he cannot drink fine French wines"
- "The Michelson-Morley experiment gave birth to a new physical theory"
- "Cancer finally caught up with him"

The book then gives the extended **INFLATION IS AN ADVERSARY** analysis (see Section 4.1 above) as a demonstration that personification is not just "inflation is a person" — it is the specific character of the person being invoked (adversary vs. ally vs. parent vs. thief) that determines the entailments.

**ADAM relevance** [ADAM analysis]. Personification is the mechanism by which brands give their products or abstractions agency and relational character. *"Your savings account is working for you," "insurance has your back," "the algorithm knows what you want"* — all are personification primes that import agent-hood into the product. Strong activators of trust, dependency, and relationship framing. Effective on users high in need for control (they want to know *who is in charge*, even if the agent is metaphorical) and on users in uncertainty (they want an ally). The choice of *which* person the abstraction is — adversary, ally, parent, teacher, friend, expert — determines the relationship the user enters with the brand.

## 6. Metonymy (Chapter 8)

Metonymy uses one entity to refer to another with which it is associated. Unlike metaphor, metonymy does not structure one conceptual domain in terms of another — it selects a salient aspect to refer to the whole. Lakoff and Johnson treat metonymy alongside metaphor because it is equally conceptually structured, equally systematic, and equally used to organize thought and action rather than just language.

> *"Metaphor is principally a way of conceiving of one thing in terms of another, and its primary function is understanding. Metonymy, on the other hand, has primarily a referential function, that is, it allows us to use one entity to stand for another. But metonymy is not merely a referential device. It also serves the function of providing understanding."* (Ch 8)

### 6.1 The systematic metonymies (Ch 8)

| Type | Example | ADAM use |
|---|---|---|
| **THE PART FOR THE WHOLE** (synecdoche) | "the automobile is clogging our highways," "we need a couple of strong bodies," "good heads in the university," "new set of wheels," "new blood in the organization" | Licenses efficient, concrete language |
| **PRODUCER FOR PRODUCT** | "he bought a Ford," "he's got a Picasso in his den," "I hate to read Heidegger" | Licenses brand-as-creator framing |
| **OBJECT USED FOR USER** | "the sax has the flu today," "the BLT is a lousy tipper," "the gun he hired wanted fifty grand," "the buses are on strike" | Licenses tool-identity framing |
| **CONTROLLER FOR CONTROLLED** | "Nixon bombed Hanoi," "Ozawa gave a terrible concert last night," "Napoleon lost at Waterloo," "a Mercedes rear-ended me" | Licenses leader-as-entity framing; assigns responsibility |
| **INSTITUTION FOR PEOPLE RESPONSIBLE** | "Exxon has raised its prices again," "you'll never get the university to agree to that," "the Army wants to reinstitute the draft," "the Senate thinks abortion is immoral" | Licenses corporate agency framing |
| **THE PLACE FOR THE INSTITUTION** | "the White House isn't saying anything," "Washington is insensitive to the needs of the people," "the Kremlin threatened to boycott," "Paris is introducing longer skirts this season," "Hollywood isn't what it used to be," "Wall Street is in a panic" | Licenses geographic shorthand for organizations |
| **THE PLACE FOR THE EVENT** | "let's not let Thailand become another Vietnam," "Remember the Alamo," "Pearl Harbor still has an effect on our foreign policy," "Watergate changed our politics" | Licenses historical-reference framing |
| **THE FACE FOR THE PERSON** | "she's just a pretty face," "there are an awful lot of faces out there in the audience," "we need some new faces around here" | Licenses identity-by-appearance framing |

Lakoff and Johnson also note that **cultural and religious symbolism is a special case of metonymy**. Their example: within Christianity, DOVE FOR HOLY SPIRIT. *"There is a reason why the dove is the symbol of the Holy Spirit and not, say, the chicken, the vulture, or the ostrich. The dove is conceived of as beautiful, friendly, gentle, and, above all, peaceful. As a bird, its natural habitat is the sky, which metonymically stands for heaven."*

**ADAM relevance** [ADAM analysis]. Metonymies are the lowest-effort priming tools available. A page that refers to "the White House" is metonymically invoking the institution; this primes authority, hierarchy, and political-structural reasoning even when the topic is adjacent. Extraction from RSS-level text is trivial and high-signal. Metonymies in *ad copy* are often about compressing agency — "your savings are working," "Wall Street trusts us" — and each compression carries a specific inferential load. **HOME STANDS FOR THE PERSON** (Ch 15) is a particularly important metonymy for any brand that traffics in residential, domestic, or belonging imagery — the home is literally standing in for the person who lives in it.

## 7. The Key Metaphors for Chris's Research Program

This section highlights the metaphors in MWLB that are most directly operationalized by Chris's own empirical priming work and the cross-linguistic universality claim.

### 7.1 PHYSICAL CLOSENESS IS PERSONAL CLOSENESS (Ch 15)

The single most directly relevant metaphor in the book for Chris's work. Lakoff and Johnson give the example:

> *"It is common in Los Angeles to engage in the ritual activity of driving by the homes of Hollywood stars. This is a metaphorical kind of activity based on the metonymy **HOME STANDS FOR THE PERSON** and the metaphor **PHYSICAL CLOSENESS IS PERSONAL CLOSENESS**."* (Ch 15)

The treatment is brief in MWLB but the metaphor is explicitly named. Chris's work extends this into the empirical demonstration that priming physical distance shifts judgments of emotional closeness on unrelated targets, and into the evolutionary claim that this is not a coincidence of linguistic convention but a consequence of how social cognition was wired on top of the existing spatial-cognition circuits during evolutionary time.

**ADAM relevance.** This metaphor is the direct ancestor of the bilateral edge dimension that should exist for *closeness* / *distance* on both the buyer side and the brand/page side. Any creative invoking proximity ("right there with you," "side by side," "up close," "intimate") is activating this substrate. Any creative invoking distance ("at arm's length," "from afar," "keeping your distance," "maintaining boundaries") is activating the opposite substrate. Page language that primes close-reading intimacy versus arm's-length detachment predicts which creative will land.

### 7.2 EMOTIONAL EFFECT IS PHYSICAL CONTACT (Ch 10)

The second most directly relevant metaphor for Chris's research program. The full list of examples from Chapter 10:

> *"His mother's death hit him hard. That idea bowled me over. She's a knockout. I was struck by his sincerity. That really made an impression on me. He made his mark on the world. I was touched by his remark. That blew me away."*

**ADAM relevance.** This is the metaphor that licenses tactile and impact verbs for emotional outcomes. A creative that says *"this will touch you"* or *"you'll be moved"* or *"hit the spot"* is invoking physical contact to activate emotional response. Chris's own priming work on physical texture influencing social-emotional judgment sits directly on top of this metaphor's substrate.

### 7.3 The UP/DOWN cluster as substrate for social hierarchy

All ten of the major UP/DOWN metaphors (Section 3.1 above) are substrate candidates for Chris's physical-to-social recycling hypothesis. HIGH STATUS IS UP, HAVING CONTROL IS UP, GOOD IS UP, HAPPY IS UP, VIRTUE IS UP, RATIONAL IS UP — every one of these is a social-emotional judgment implemented on the embodied machinery of verticality. The experimental prediction is that priming physical verticality (standing tall, looking up, elevated viewpoint) should shift social-emotional judgments on unrelated targets along the corresponding UP/DOWN axis. This has been demonstrated empirically in the broader priming literature; MWLB provides the conceptual map of exactly which social judgments should be affected by verticality priming.

### 7.4 Container metaphors as substrate for belonging and safety

LIFE IS A CONTAINER, STATES ARE CONTAINERS, SOCIAL GROUPS ARE CONTAINERS, THE MIND IS A CONTAINER — the full container cluster — are all candidates for priming experiments where physical enclosure (wrapping, holding, bounded space) shifts social-emotional judgments of belonging, safety, protection, and inclusion. The WEALTH IS A HIDDEN OBJECT metaphor adds a specific containment subcase: wealth is something *inside* a container that must be discovered. Priming physical containment / enclosure should shift perceived in-group membership and safety judgments.

### 7.5 The recycling-substrate predictions implied by MWLB

Pulling together the primary metaphors from MWLB that directly invoke physical-to-social substrate recycling, the full set of axes Chris's research program can be mapped against:

| Axis | MWLB metaphor | Physical pole primes | Social-emotional outcome |
|---|---|---|---|
| Vertical | HAPPY IS UP, HEALTH IS UP, CONTROL IS UP, STATUS IS UP, GOOD IS UP, VIRTUE IS UP, RATIONAL IS UP | standing tall, looking up, elevated viewpoint | improved mood, perceived status, rational engagement, virtue attribution |
| Closeness | PHYSICAL CLOSENESS IS PERSONAL CLOSENESS, CLOSENESS IS STRENGTH OF EFFECT, SIMILARITY IS CLOSENESS | physical proximity to person, image, or object | emotional intimacy, similarity judgment, perceived impact |
| Touch/contact | EMOTIONAL EFFECT IS PHYSICAL CONTACT, SEEING IS TOUCHING, EYES ARE LIMBS | haptic contact, visual attention as reaching | emotional intensity, engagement, impression formation |
| Containment | STATES ARE CONTAINERS, LIFE IS A CONTAINER, SOCIAL GROUPS ARE CONTAINERS | physical enclosure, wrapping, bounded space | belonging, safety, in-group membership |
| Size | SIGNIFICANT IS BIG, MORE IS UP | physical size of stimulus | perceived importance, significance |
| Weight | IDEAS ARE OBJECTS (implicit), problems as burdens | physical weight of object | perceived seriousness, importance, trust |
| Solidity | THEORIES ARE BUILDINGS | physical solidity (rock, stone, foundation) | reliability, trust, stability |
| Temperature | (not explicit in MWLB 1980) | warmth vs. cold | AFFECTION IS WARMTH — added in later Lakoff/Johnson work and primary metaphor theory, empirically demonstrated by Williams & Bargh 2008 |
| Brightness | UNDERSTANDING IS SEEING, IDEAS ARE LIGHT-SOURCES | physical brightness | clarity, understanding, virtue |
| Depth | "profound" vs "shallow" | physical depth | authenticity, substance, seriousness |

**Note.** Temperature and texture are not explicitly given their own named metaphors in MWLB 1980 (AFFECTION IS WARMTH and COOPERATION IS SMOOTHNESS appear in later primary-metaphor-theory work by Grady and in Lakoff & Johnson's *Philosophy in the Flesh*, 1999). The book does include EMOTIONAL EFFECT IS PHYSICAL CONTACT, which gets at the tactile dimension indirectly. Chris's own cross-linguistic survey work appears to have verified temperature and texture as universal primary metaphors even though they are not named as such in MWLB 1980.

## 9. Cautions and Open Questions

### 9.1 Coherence is more important than consistency

A major point from Chapter 9 that has implications for ADAM's retargeting design: Lakoff and Johnson explicitly argue that different metaphors for the same concept can *coexist without being consistent*, as long as they are *coherent* (share major entailments). The several LOVE metaphors do not form one consistent image, but they all license the same major entailment that love involves relationship structure between two people.

**Implication for ADAM** [ADAM analysis]: the "metaphor coherence constraint" I proposed for retargeting sequences (Section 2.4 of the theoretical foundation) needs to be **coherence-based, not consistency-based**. Successive impressions do not need to invoke the same metaphor — they need to invoke coherent metaphors that share major entailments. A sequence that invokes LOVE IS A JOURNEY and then LOVE IS A PATIENT is coherent (both license care and long-term investment); a sequence that invokes LOVE IS A JOURNEY and then LOVE IS WAR is incoherent (adversarial vs. cooperative). The constraint is about shared entailments, not shared images.

### 9.2 What this file is NOT

- It is not a complete enumeration of every metaphor in the book. Chapters 12–30 cover experiential basis, causation, coherent structuring, definition, truth, and the objectivism/subjectivism debate — these contain fewer explicit metaphor statements but extensive discussion that future sessions may want to consult.
- It does not cover the **2003 afterword**, which integrates Joseph Grady's primary metaphor theory and several later developments. The afterword is in the PDF and should be read as a supplement when the creative engine work begins.
- It does not yet ground the metaphor axes against ADAM's existing 27-dimension bilateral edge schema. That audit is a prerequisite for the schema extension work and has not been done.

### 9.3 Open empirical questions for ADAM

- **Which primary metaphor axes have the strongest priming effects in advertising contexts specifically?** Williams & Bargh (Science 2008) is the canonical warmth demonstration; other axes have been tested with varying replication records. Before committing all 10 substrate axes from Section 8.1 as first-class edge dimensions, a literature review of *consumer-judgment* priming effects specifically would scope which are highest value.
- **Do metaphor-axis alignments compose linearly, or are there interaction effects?** Testable once the dimensions exist.
- **Temporal profile of metaphor priming.** Primary metaphor primes decay (the warmth-cup effect is minutes, not hours). How this interacts with the PK/PD retargeting model I proposed is an open question with testable predictions.
