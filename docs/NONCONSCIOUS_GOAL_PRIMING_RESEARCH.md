# Nonconscious Goal Priming: Theoretical Foundation for INFORMATIV

## Research compiled April 12, 2026

This document synthesizes the research literature on nonconscious goal priming
as it applies to the INFORMATIV crossover page targeting system.

---

## THE 7-STAGE MECHANISM CHAIN

### Stage 1: Content-Goal Activation
Page content serves as the situational context (Bargh 1990 auto-motive model).
Semantic features of the text, images, and topic activate goal representations
through learned situation-goal associations. This activation is:
- Nonconscious (Bargh & Chartrand, 1999; Dijksterhuis & Aarts, 2010)
- Automatic -- within milliseconds of content exposure
- Goal-specific -- different content activates different goals based on
  the reader's personal history of situation-goal pairings

### Stage 2: Affective Charging
Content valence determines motivational potency (Custers & Aarts, 2010).
Positive-affect content produces more motivationally charged goal activation.
The activated goal acquires "wanting" properties from the positive affect.

### Stage 3: Goal Shielding
The activated goal inhibits competing goals (Shah, 2003; Forster et al., 2007).
The user's motivational landscape narrows: one goal is active and energized,
competing goals are suppressed. This creates a receptive window for
goal-consistent ads and a hostile environment for goal-inconsistent ads.

### Stage 4: Attentional Reorientation
The active goal redirects perceptual attention to goal-relevant stimuli
(Dijksterhuis & Aarts, 2010; Papies, 2016). The user's eyes and cognitive
processing are biased toward elements semantically connected to the active goal.
This happens without awareness (goals need attention, not awareness).

### Stage 5: Ad Encounter as Goal-Fulfillment Opportunity
The ad enters a psychological field already organized by the active goal.
If the ad offers a means to the active goal (Kruglanski goal systems theory):
- Transferred motivation from the goal to the means
- Preferential attention from goal-directed attentional bias
- Processing fluency from semantic congruence
- Positive affect misattributed from goal-content fluency to the ad/brand

### Stage 6: Goal-Consistent Choice
The primed goal shifts choice toward goal-consistent options (Chartrand et al., 2008):
- Intensifies over moderate delay (unlike semantic priming, which decays)
- Reduced by prior satiation (recent purchases reduce the effect)
- Proportional to goal value (more important goals produce stronger effects)
- Shows equifinality -- flexible means selection

### Stage 7: Post-Click Goal Persistence
The nonconscious goal persists after initial engagement (Bargh et al., 2001).
Click-through users carry the active goal into the landing page and subsequent
funnel. The goal increases in strength as attainment approaches (distance gradient).

## GOAL PRIMING vs CONCEPT/TRAIT PRIMING (Forster et al., 2007)

| Property | Goal Priming | Concept Priming |
|----------|-------------|-----------------|
| Value moderation | Effects stronger for more valued goals | No value moderation |
| Post-attainment | Effects diminish after goal achieved | No satiation |
| Distance gradient | Effects intensify near attainment | No distance sensitivity |
| Expectancy x Value | Effects proportional to E×V | Not moderated |
| Competing inhibition | Active goal suppresses competitors | No inhibition |
| Self-control | Can override competing impulses | No self-regulatory properties |
| Equifinality | Flexible means selection | Fixed response |

## 10 DESIGN PRINCIPLES

1. **Goal classification, not topic classification.** Classify content by goals
   activated, not by topic. "Home renovation" is a topic; "improve my living
   environment" is a goal.

2. **Temporal dynamics.** Goal effects intensify over moderate delays
   (Chartrand et al., 2008). Ads served 30-60 seconds after priming content
   may outperform simultaneous ads.

3. **Satiation modeling.** Recent purchases satiate goals. Purchase history
   should dampen goal-relevance scores (Chartrand et al., 2008).

4. **Positive affect amplifies.** Content with positive emotional valence
   makes activated goals more motivationally potent (Custers & Aarts, 2010).

5. **Avoid triggering persuasion knowledge.** Ads perceived as persuasion
   attempts trigger correction goals that reverse effects (Laran et al., 2011).

6. **Goal shielding = double penalty for mismatched ads.** Mismatched ads are
   not just ignored but actively suppressed (Shah, 2003).

7. **Self-concept discrepancy predicts goal vs semantic activation.**
   Aspirational themes activate goals; confirmed-identity themes activate
   concepts only (Sela & Shiv, 2009).

8. **Goal contagion from narrative content.** Content featuring people pursuing
   goals transmits those goals through contagion (Aarts et al., 2004).

9. **Specificity beats breadth.** Due to dilution in goal systems (Kruglanski),
   narrow value propositions aligned to one goal receive more transferred
   motivation than broad multi-benefit ads.

10. **The unconscious does the heavy lifting.** The entire chain operates without
    conscious mediation (Hassin, 2013). Self-report data on "why I clicked"
    will be unreliable. Validate on behavioral outcomes.

## META-ANALYTIC EVIDENCE

- Chen et al. (2021): d = 0.44 for achievement goal priming on performance
- Dai & Albarracin (2023): d = 0.332-0.352 across 352 effect sizes, 133 studies
- Field experiments: d = 0.67 (stronger than laboratory)
- Latham field experiments: 15% effectiveness, 35% efficiency improvement
